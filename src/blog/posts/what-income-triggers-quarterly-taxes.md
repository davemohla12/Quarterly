## Why Quarterly Taxes Exist

The U.S. tax system is “pay-as-you-go.” That means the IRS expects you to pay taxes **as you earn income**, not just once a year in April.

If you don’t have taxes withheld from your income — like W-2 employees do — the IRS wants you to send in estimated payments **four times a year**.

## The $1,000 Rule

If you expect to owe **$1,000 or more in federal tax** this year — and your income doesn’t have taxes withheld — the IRS requires you to make quarterly payments.

✅ If you’re self-employed, a contractor, or earning untaxed income from any source, this likely applies to you.

## Common Income Sources That Trigger Quarterly Taxes

Here’s what typically requires estimated tax payments:

- ✅ **Freelance income** (1099, contract work)  
- ✅ **Side hustle income** (coaching, tutoring, etc.)  
- ✅ **Content creator income** (YouTube, Substack, Gumroad)  
- ✅ **Digital product or course sales**  
- ✅ **Affiliate marketing or sponsorships**  
- ✅ **Tips or cash payments**  
- ✅ **Rental income**  
- ✅ **Etsy, Shopify, or eBay sales**  
- ✅ **Crypto or stock gains** (if you sell)  
- ✅ **Business income from an LLC or sole proprietorship**  
- ✅ **Interest or dividends (if large enough)**

If **no taxes are withheld**, and you expect to owe over $1,000 for the year — quarterly taxes are required.

## What About W-2 Jobs?

If your only income comes from a W-2 job and taxes are withheld, you probably don’t need to worry about quarterly taxes.

But if you **also** earn untaxed side income — even a few thousand dollars — you may need to pay quarterly taxes on that part.

✅ Example:  
- $70,000 salary (withheld)  
- $12,000 freelance work (not withheld)  
→ You may need to make quarterly payments on the $12,000

## What If You’re Just Starting Out?

Even first-year freelancers may owe quarterly taxes.

✅ If your side income grows fast, or you land a big client, that income can quickly add up — and trigger the $1,000 threshold.

To stay on the safe side:

- Estimate your annual income  
- Subtract business expenses  
- If the tax owed is $1,000+, plan to pay quarterly

## How to Avoid Surprises

- ✅ Track your income as you go  
- ✅ Set aside 25–30% of profits for taxes  
- ✅ Recalculate each quarter if income changes  
- ✅ Use a calculator or app to stay on target

## What Happens If You Miss It?

If you should have made quarterly payments and didn’t, the IRS may charge:

- ✅ **Underpayment penalties**  
- ✅ **Late payment interest**  
- ✅ **Fees** if you miss deadlines

These fees can stack up — even if you pay everything in full at tax time.
