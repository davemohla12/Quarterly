# Avoiding IRS Penalties: What Happens If You Miss a Quarterly Tax Payment?
<div style="margin-top: 5px; margin-bottom: 20px;">March 15, 2026</div>

**Missed a quarterly tax payment or worried you might? Here’s what actually happens, how much it can cost you, and what you can do to avoid IRS penalties.**

## Why the IRS Requires Quarterly Taxes

When you’re self-employed, the IRS doesn’t get taxes automatically taken from your paycheck.  

Instead, they expect you to **pay as you go** — four times a year — to cover:

- Federal income tax
- Self-employment tax (Social Security + Medicare)
- Sometimes state income tax

If you don’t pay enough or pay late, they apply penalties.

## What Are the IRS Penalties for Missing Quarterly Taxes?

✅ **Late payment penalty:**  
The IRS charges ~0.5% of the unpaid amount **per month** it’s late.

✅ **Underpayment penalty:**  
If you underpay across the year, the IRS may add an extra penalty — even if you pay in full at tax time.

✅ **Interest charges:**  
The IRS also charges interest on the unpaid amount, which compounds over time.

## How Big Are the Penalties?

While the percentages sound small, they can **add up fast** — especially if you miss multiple quarters.

For example, missing a $3,000 quarterly payment could lead to:

- ~$15/month penalty  
- Interest charges that grow over time

Over a year, that can mean **hundreds of dollars in extra fees**.

## What If You Pay Everything at Year-End?

✅ Even if you pay your full tax bill when you file, you can still face **underpayment penalties** for not paying quarterly.

The IRS expects you to pay **at least 90% of your total tax** during the year — or 100% of last year’s tax, whichever is lower.

## How to Avoid Penalties

- **Mark your calendar** for quarterly deadlines  
- **Set aside 25–30%** of income as you earn it  
- **Use a calculator or app** to stay on track  
- **Catch up quickly** if you miss a payment — the sooner you pay, the less penalty you owe

## What If You Missed a Payment?

✅ Don’t panic — but don’t ignore it.

- Make the payment **as soon as possible**  
- Include any owed penalty + interest  
- Adjust your next payments to stay on track

If you underpaid due to a valid reason (like a natural disaster), you may qualify for a penalty waiver — check IRS Form 2210.
