## Why You Might Not Need an Accountant

Quarterly taxes feel intimidating — but for most freelancers and solo business owners, you don’t need to hire a pro just to figure them out.

✅ With a little guidance, you can calculate them yourself in just a few minutes each quarter.

All you need is:

- Your income  
- Your expenses  
- A calculator or tax tool

## Step 1: Estimate Your Total Income for the Year

Look at how much you expect to earn in total this year from your freelance work or business.

✅ Include:

- Client payments  
- Side hustle income  
- 1099s, Venmo, or cash payments  
- Digital product or subscription sales

Use last year’s income as a baseline if you’re unsure.

## Step 2: Subtract Business Expenses

Take your income and subtract any deductible business expenses. This gives you your **net income** (aka profit).

✅ Common deductions include:

- Software and tools  
- Equipment and office supplies  
- Home office expenses  
- Travel and meals  
- Marketing and advertising  
- Contractor or virtual assistant costs

Be honest and reasonable — the IRS expects legitimate business use.

## Step 3: Apply a Tax Rate

Now that you have your profit, apply a blended tax rate to estimate what you’ll owe.

✅ A good rule of thumb:

- 15.3% for self-employment tax  
- 10–24% for federal income tax (depends on your total income and deductions)

Most freelancers should set aside **25–30%** of their profit for taxes.

Example:

- Net income: $60,000  
- 25% tax rate  
- Estimated tax: $15,000

## Step 4: Divide by 4

Since the IRS wants taxes paid quarterly, divide your total estimate by four.

✅ In this example:  
$15,000 ÷ 4 = $3,750 per quarter

This is the amount you should send each quarter — unless your income significantly changes during the year (see below).

## Step 5: Check for State Taxes

If you live in a state with income tax, you may need to send quarterly payments to your **state** as well.

✅ Use your state’s tax website or a calculator that includes state estimates.

Some states (like California and New York) require it if you owe even $300–500. Others (like Florida or Texas) have no state income tax at all.

## Step 6: Recalculate as Income Changes

If your income changes throughout the year — and it probably will — just update your estimate.

✅ Earn more than expected? Recalculate and increase next quarter’s payments  
✅ Earn less? Adjust your payments down to avoid overpaying

Quarterly taxes are flexible. The key is keeping them accurate as your income evolves.

## Step 7: Pay Online

✅ Pay your federal taxes at [IRS Direct Pay](https://www.irs.gov/payments/direct-pay)  
✅ Use your state’s payment portal if required

You don’t need to mail anything — it’s fast, easy, and you get confirmation instantly.
