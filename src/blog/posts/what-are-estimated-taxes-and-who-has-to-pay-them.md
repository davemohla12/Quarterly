## What Are Estimated Taxes?

When you’re self-employed, no one withholds taxes from your paycheck like a regular employer would.

That means you’re responsible for paying taxes **as you earn income** — not just once a year.

✅ **Estimated taxes** are payments you send the IRS (and sometimes your state) **four times a year** to cover:

- Federal income tax  
- Self-employment tax (Social Security + Medicare)  
- State income tax (if applicable)

Think of it as "pay-as-you-go" for taxes.

## Who Has to Pay Estimated Taxes?

If you’re earning income without tax being withheld — and you expect to owe **at least $1,000** in taxes for the year — the IRS wants you to pay quarterly.

This includes:

- ✅ Freelancers and consultants  
- ✅ Indie creators (Substack, Gumroad, YouTube, etc.)  
- ✅ Small business owners and solopreneurs  
- ✅ Contractors or gig workers (Uber, DoorDash, etc.)  
- ✅ Side hustlers with growing income  

Even if it’s not your full-time job, you may still owe estimated taxes if your side income is significant.

## What Income Triggers Estimated Taxes?

You may need to pay estimated taxes if you earn money from:

- 1099 income (freelance or contract work)  
- Cash or Venmo payments  
- Digital product sales  
- Subscriptions or memberships  
- Rental income  
- Tips or commissions  
- Cryptocurrency or investment gains  
- Any business income without tax withheld

Basically: if it’s income and no one’s withholding taxes, you likely owe estimated taxes.

## What If You Also Have a W-2 Job?

If you have a regular job with tax withheld **and** a side hustle — you still might need to pay estimated taxes on your side income.

✅ Example:  
- You earn $60,000 at your job (with taxes withheld)  
- You earn $15,000 on the side (no taxes withheld)  

You may owe quarterly taxes on that $15,000.

## When Are Estimated Taxes Due?

The IRS has four quarterly deadlines each year:

- **April 15** (for Jan–Mar income)  
- **June 15** (for Apr–May income)  
- **September 15** (for Jun–Aug income)  
- **January 15** of the next year (for Sep–Dec income)

Some states follow the same schedule, while others vary slightly — always check.

## What Happens If You Don’t Pay?

If you skip estimated tax payments or don’t pay enough, the IRS can hit you with:

- ✅ **Late payment penalties** (0.5% per month)  
- ✅ **Underpayment penalties** at year-end  
- ✅ **Interest** on the unpaid amount

Even if you pay your full tax bill in April, you may still owe penalties if you didn’t pay enough during the year.

## Why Estimated Taxes Matter

Staying on top of estimated taxes helps you:

- Avoid penalties and interest  
- Stay in control of your cash flow  
- Prevent surprises during tax season  
- Build peace of mind as your income grows

You’re building something valuable — don’t let taxes trip you up.
