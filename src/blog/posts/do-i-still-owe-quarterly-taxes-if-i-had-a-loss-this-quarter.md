## Quarterly Taxes and Business Losses

Quarterly estimated taxes are based on what you **expect to owe for the full year**, not just one quarter.

✅ So if you had a **loss this quarter** — but still expect to make a profit this year — you may still owe taxes.

It all depends on your **annual outlook**.

## When You *Don’t* Have to Pay

If you had a loss this quarter **and** you expect your **total net income for the year to be zero or negative**, you likely **don’t owe** quarterly taxes.

✅ Examples:

- You spent more than you earned and expect that to continue  
- You shut down your business mid-year with no future income  
- You had a temporary pause and no income is expected going forward

If you don’t expect to owe **at least $1,000** in tax for the year, you’re not required to make estimated payments.

## When You *Still* Might Owe

If your business had a loss this quarter, but:

- ✅ You expect to rebound in Q3 or Q4  
- ✅ You made a profit in a previous quarter  
- ✅ Your **overall yearly income** will still be positive  

…then you may still need to make estimated payments in future quarters.

IRS penalties are based on **underpayment for the full year**, not one specific quarter.

## How the IRS Looks at It

The IRS wants to see that you paid at least:

- **90% of what you’ll owe this year**, or  
- **100% of last year’s tax** (110% if you made over $150K)

✅ If you’re on track to meet either of those “safe harbor” rules, you’re usually fine — even if you skip a quarter due to temporary loss.

## What You Should Do

- ✅ Recalculate your estimated taxes each quarter  
- ✅ If you expect to earn more later, adjust your next payments upward  
- ✅ Don’t overpay now just to “stay safe” — it’s better to pay based on real income

Example:

- Q1: $5,000 profit → $1,250 tax  
- Q2: $3,000 loss → no tax  
- Q3: $8,000 profit → recalculate and pay for Q2 + Q3 combined

The IRS only cares that enough was paid by **year-end**, not that every quarter was identical.

## How to Handle Catch-Up Payments

If you skipped or reduced a payment in one quarter, you can:

- ✅ Increase your next payment to make up for it
