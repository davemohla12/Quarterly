## Why Do You Need to Estimate Quarterly Taxes?

When you’re self-employed, no one withholds taxes from your paychecks. That means you’re responsible for paying:

- Federal income tax
- Self-employment tax (Social Security + Medicare)
- Possibly state income tax

The IRS wants you to estimate and pay these taxes four times a year — based on what you’re earning as you go.

## Step 1: Estimate Your Total Annual Income

Look at your expected income for the year.

✅ Add up freelance contracts, consulting income, side hustles, tips — everything.

If your income fluctuates, make your best estimate for the year.

## Step 2: Subtract Business Expenses and Deductions

✅ Subtract all legitimate business expenses:
- Software, equipment, supplies
- Home office portion
- Travel, meals, marketing

This gives you your **taxable profit**.

## Step 3: Calculate Your Taxes

✅ Use an estimated tax rate:
- ~15.3% self-employment tax
- Plus your federal income tax bracket (10–24% for most freelancers)

As a rule of thumb, set aside **25–30% of your profit** to cover both.

## Step 4: Divide by Four

✅ Divide the total estimated tax by four.

This is the amount you should send to the IRS each quarter.

Example:

Estimated tax for the year: $12,000  
Quarterly payment: $12,000 ÷ 4 = $3,000 per quarter

## Step 5: Check State Requirements

Some states require separate estimated tax payments.  
✅ Look up your state rules or use a calculator that includes state taxes.

## What If Your Income Changes During the Year?

✅ Recalculate!

Quarterly taxes are based on what you *expect* to owe — so if your income jumps or drops, update your estimate for the next quarter.

## Why Use a Calculator or App?

Manually estimating quarterly taxes can be stressful and error-prone.

✅ A good calculator or app:
- Handles self-employment tax + income tax
- Includes federal + state
- Updates when you change your numbers

## Avoiding Penalties

The IRS expects you to pay **at least 90% of what you owe** by year-end (or 100% of last year’s tax).

✅ If you underpay, you could face penalties — even if you pay the full amount in April.
